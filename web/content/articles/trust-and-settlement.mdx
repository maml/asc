# Trust and Settlement: Why the Right Tools for Agent Coordination Are Not the Ones You'd Reach For

*I'm building [ASC](https://github.com/maml/asc) (Agentic Services Coordinator). In the first article, I laid out [The Coordination Gap](/blog/the-coordination-gap) — the missing infrastructure layer between organizations where agent-to-agent service delivery has no standards, no quality enforcement, and no governance. I identified eight capabilities that coordination layer needs. This piece drills into two of them — trust and settlement — and makes the engineering case for why the obvious tools to solve them are the wrong tools.*

---

In [The Coordination Gap](/blog/the-coordination-gap) we outlined the eight capabilities the coordination layer needs: routing, handoffs, SLAs, quality gates, billing, observability, trust, and governance.

Two of those eight — **trust** and **settlement** — have a deeper problem that most people in the agent infrastructure space haven't thought through yet.

Engineers definitely have an instinct for how to solve them. Use OAuth. Use Stripe. Use X.509 certificates. Use ACH. All reasonable. I had the same ones. But when you write down what cross-organizational agent coordination actually requires — and then evaluate each tool against those requirements — you end up somewhere unexpected.

The tools you'd reach for first are the wrong tools.

Here's why.

## The delegation nightmare

Here is the scenario that should keep every agent infrastructure engineer awake at night.

Agent A belongs to Organization 1. It receives a request from a consumer. Agent A determines it needs a specialized capability it doesn't have, so it delegates to Agent B at Organization 2, through a coordination layer.

Agent B now inherits Agent A's trust chain. But B has zero independent verification. B doesn't know who the original consumer was. B doesn't know whether A was actually authorized to delegate this type of request. B doesn't know whether the delegation chain is legitimate or whether it was fabricated by a compromised intermediary.

This is not a theoretical problem. This is a known, documented problem in service mesh infrastructure.

**SPIFFE** (Secure Production Identity Framework for Everyone) exists specifically because this delegation problem appeared in Kubernetes, Istio, and HashiCorp Consul deployments. When services call other services that call other services, the downstream service has no way to verify the full chain of trust. SPIFFE addressed it for microservices within a cluster. Nobody has addressed it for agents across organizations.

Now scale this. Ten organizations. Fifty agents. Delegation chains three or four levels deep. Every link in the chain is a trust assumption with no cryptographic backing. One compromised agent in the middle of a chain can impersonate any upstream agent and redirect downstream work.

The coordination layer sits exactly where this problem lives — between organizations. It is the right place to solve it. But the solution has to meet specific requirements.

## Three models for trust

There are three ways to approach trust in a cross-organizational agent coordination system. Only one of them survives contact with production.

### Model 1: Centralized authority

The coordination platform issues all identities. The platform signs everything. The platform is the root of trust.

This is the simplest model. It is also "trust me, bro" scaled to an entire ecosystem.

A single point of trust failure means platform compromise equals ecosystem compromise. Every provider depends on the platform's signature — no independent verification path exists. Regulators examining cross-organizational AI interactions will find a single chokepoint with no redundancy. And every organization's identity is locked to one vendor at the **trust layer**, which is the hardest layer to migrate away from. You can switch payment processors in a quarter. Migrating your organizational identity is a multi-year project.

### Model 2: Zero trust / sovereign identity

Each organization holds its own keypair. They register their public key with the coordination layer. They sign their own requests. No single point of trust failure.

This model has real strengths. **Trust is portable** — if the coordination platform disappears, organizations still control their own identities. Regulators can verify any organization independently. No vendor lock-in at the trust layer.

But it has real costs. Onboarding is complex — every organization has to manage its own key infrastructure. Distributed revocation is hard (as anyone who has operated a PKI at scale will confirm). And you still need some trust anchor to bootstrap the system. Someone has to attest that this public key actually belongs to this organization.

### Model 3: Hybrid — trust broker, not trust source

The coordination layer does not issue identities. Organizations bring their own keypairs. The coordination layer **attests to relationships** — it confirms that Org A and Org B have a service agreement, that Org A has authorized this delegation scope, that this agent's key belongs to this organization.

Coordination tokens carry multiple signatures. The requesting org signs. The coordination layer co-signs the relationship attestation. The providing org can verify both independently.

Any party can verify any other party without depending on the platform.

Here is the critical property: **platform compromise can deny service but cannot impersonate organizations.** If the coordination layer is breached, the attacker can prevent coordination tokens from being issued. They cannot forge an organization's signature. They cannot create delegation chains that pass independent verification. The blast radius of a breach shrinks from "the entire trust model collapses" to "service is interrupted until the platform recovers."

This is the right model. It is the only model that survives both a security audit and a regulatory review for cross-organizational AI governance.

## Why traditional auth fails

Now that we know the trust model, we need cryptographic primitives to implement it. The instinct is to reach for familiar tools. Let's evaluate them.

### X.509 / PKI

X.509 certificates are the backbone of TLS. They're battle-tested. They're everywhere. And they are a poor fit for this problem.

X.509 requires a **Certificate Authority** — a centralized trust dependency. That conflicts directly with the hybrid model above. Organizations cannot self-provision identities; they must request certificates from a CA. Revocation infrastructure (CRL and OCSP) is a documented operational mess — CRLs grow unbounded, OCSP adds latency and availability dependencies, and neither works reliably at the scale of thousands of ephemeral agent-to-agent interactions per second.

X.509 was designed for long-lived server identities, not for high-frequency, short-lived delegation chains between autonomous agents.

### OAuth / API Keys

OAuth assumes a trusted issuer. The identity is only as sovereign as the platform that issued the token. Revoke the platform, revoke the identity. That is centralized trust with extra steps.

API keys are shared secrets. Shared secrets collapse **trust boundaries** by definition — if two parties share a secret, neither can prove which one used it. No delegation chain verification. No independent verification. No auditability of who did what.

### What the requirements actually demand

Strip away the tool names and write down what cross-organizational agent trust actually needs:

- **Self-sovereign keypairs** — orgs generate and control their own identity
- **Message signing without a CA** — any party can sign, any party can verify, no central issuer
- **Verifiable delegation chains** — each link in a delegation chain is independently provable
- **Agent-operable** — agents can generate keys, sign messages, and verify signatures without human intervention
- **High-frequency capable** — thousands of signing operations per second, not certificate issuance workflows

That requirements list eliminates X.509, OAuth, and API keys. Not because they're bad tools. Because they were built for different problems.

## The payment problem

Trust is half the story. The other half is settlement.

When Agent A at Org 1 consumes a service from Agent B at Org 2, someone has to pay. Usage-based, in near real-time, across organizational and potentially national boundaries.

Here is how the traditional settlement stack compares to what agent-to-agent coordination actually requires:

| Requirement | Stripe / ACH | Agent-to-Agent Settlement Needs |
|---|---|---|
| **Minimum transaction** | ~$0.30-0.50 fee floor | Sub-cent payments (agent tasks cost fractions of a penny) |
| **Settlement speed** | 1-3 business days (ACH), seconds (card) | Milliseconds (agents don't wait for batch settlement) |
| **Availability** | Business hours for ACH, 24/7 for cards with maintenance windows | 24/7/365, no exceptions (agents don't observe bank holidays) |
| **Cross-border** | Complex — currency conversion, correspondent banking, compliance per jurisdiction | Borderless by default |
| **Programmability** | API-native (Stripe is excellent here) | API-native, agent-operable |
| **Account requirements** | Business bank account, KYC, incorporation docs | Permissionless — agents can't do KYC |
| **Dispute model** | Chargebacks (60-120 day windows, designed for human commerce) | Programmatic resolution (milliseconds, not months) |
| **Who can hold the credential** | A human who passed identity verification | Any entity that can hold a private key |

Read that last row again.

**An AI agent can hold a private key. It cannot open a Chase checking account.**

That is not a philosophical argument. That is an engineering constraint. If your settlement layer requires a bank account, you have built a system that agents structurally cannot operate. You need a human in the loop for every financial relationship, at every organizational boundary, forever. That is not a coordination layer. That is a bottleneck with a billing API.

The fee floor problem is equally disqualifying. Agent-to-agent interactions will often cost fractions of a cent. A research agent that queries three provider agents to synthesize a response might generate $0.002 in value per provider. Stripe's minimum viable transaction makes this economically impossible. You'd spend more on the payment than the service costs.

## The tool that fits

Here is where the process of elimination leads.

The trust requirements demand: self-sovereign keypairs, message signing without a CA, verifiable delegation chains, agent-operable. The settlement requirements demand: sub-cent payments, millisecond settlement, permissionless, borderless, 24/7, agent-operable.

One cryptographic ecosystem was built from the ground up to solve exactly these two problems simultaneously: **permissionless cryptographic identity** and **permissionless value transfer.**

The keypair scheme is **secp256k1** — the same elliptic curve cryptography used across production systems worldwide. Every wallet is fundamentally a keypair. The private key signs. The public key verifies. No certificate authority. No issuing platform. No central dependency.

- **Self-sovereign by default.** An organization generates a keypair. That keypair is their identity. No one issued it. No one can revoke it without the private key. It satisfies the hybrid trust model natively.
- **Message signing is built in and battle-tested.** Hundreds of billions of dollars in value have been secured by these signatures. The signing and verification primitives are fast, well-audited, and available in every programming language.
- **HD key derivation (BIP-32)** solves the delegation problem cleanly. From a single master key, an organization can derive scoped child keys — one per agent, one per relationship, one per delegation scope. Compromise of a child key does not compromise the master. Revocation of a child key is a local operation. An agent gets exactly the key scope it needs and nothing more. This is the cryptographic primitive that makes verifiable delegation chains practical.

For settlement: the **Lightning Network.**

- **Millisecond settlement.** Payment confirmation in the time it takes to complete an API call. No batch processing. No business-hours dependency.
- **Sub-cent precision.** The minimum unit is 1 satoshi — approximately $0.00001 at current prices. Agent tasks that generate fractions of a penny in value can be settled individually, in real time.
- **Programmable.** Lightning invoices are data structures. Agents can create them, pay them, and verify them without human intervention.
- **Borderless.** No correspondent banking. No currency conversion intermediaries. No jurisdiction-specific payment rails.
- **Permissionless.** An agent that can hold a private key can operate a Lightning node or wallet. No bank account. No KYC. No incorporation documents.
- **24/7/365.** No maintenance windows. No bank holidays. No settlement cut-off times.

And then there is the mechanism that makes production-grade escrow possible without a trusted intermediary: **hold invoices.** Funds are locked in a payment channel. The agent task executes. A quality gate validates the output. If the output passes, the payment is released automatically. If it fails, the payment is cancelled. The coordination layer enforces the quality gate. Neither party holds the other's money. No escrow agent. No dispute window measured in months. Programmatic settlement in milliseconds, conditioned on verifiable task completion.

That is escrow with no intermediary holding funds. It is the settlement primitive that agent-to-agent SLAs require.

## The convergence

Here is the architectural property that no other stack offers.

The same secp256k1 keypair that authenticates a request **controls the payment channel** for that request. Identity and settlement unify on the same cryptographic primitive.

There is no separate "auth system" and "billing system." The key that proves "this request came from Org A's agent" is the same key that authorizes "pay Org B 500 satoshis for this completed task." One keypair. One verification. One trust model that spans both identity and value transfer.

This is a meaningful architectural simplification. Every additional system in a cross-organizational trust chain is an additional attack surface, an additional failure mode, and an additional vendor dependency. Collapsing identity and settlement onto a single primitive reduces all three.

## Honest risks

I would not trust an argument that didn't name its own weaknesses.

**Volatility.** Bitcoin's price moves. The answer is straightforward: price services in USD, settle in satoshis at spot rate at the moment of settlement. The coordination layer quotes in fiat. The settlement rail converts at execution. Organizations that want price stability get it. The settlement layer handles the conversion transparently.

**Enterprise adoption friction.** Many enterprises are not ready to interact with Bitcoin infrastructure directly. The coordination layer should offer this as one settlement option alongside traditional billing for organizations that prefer it. The architecture supports both. Over time, the economic advantages of sub-cent settlement will drive adoption on their own.

**Regulatory considerations.** The regulatory landscape for digital asset settlement varies by jurisdiction. This is real and requires compliance engineering. It is also a solved problem — regulated custodians, compliant payment processors, and clear frameworks exist in major markets. The Lightning Network's characteristics (instant settlement, no custodial requirement) actually simplify several regulatory concerns compared to traditional crypto settlement.

**Liquidity management.** Organizations operating Lightning channels need to manage channel capacity. This is an operational requirement, not a fundamental limitation. Channel management tooling has matured significantly, and the coordination layer can abstract most of this complexity from end users.

These are engineering problems with known solutions. They are not dealbreakers.

## Where this leaves us

The coordination layer is the right place to enforce trust and settlement because it sits between organizations. It is the boundary where identity must be verified, delegation must be proven, and value must change hands.

The tool you reach for should match the job, not the brand.

When you write down the requirements — sovereign identity, message signing without a central authority, verifiable delegation chains, sub-cent settlement, millisecond finality, agent-operable, permissionless, borderless, always-on — and then evaluate every available tool against that list, the process of elimination is clear.

The primitives that survive are secp256k1 keypairs and Lightning Network settlement. They happen to come from an ecosystem that solved "permissionless cryptographic identity plus value transfer" fifteen years ago. That is not an ideological statement. That is an engineering observation.

The agent services economy will not wait for traditional financial infrastructure to adapt. It will not wait for certificate authorities to design agent-friendly issuance workflows. It will not wait for ACH to support sub-cent transactions. The agents are already running. The delegation chains are already forming. The coordination gap is already costing real money.

The infrastructure that fills that gap needs to match the speed, the scale, and the operational reality of the systems it coordinates.

The requirements are specific. The tools that meet them are specific. The rest is execution.
